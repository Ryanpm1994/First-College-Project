Basic Knowledge

Numbers are always a useful thing to know - for quantities, measurements, distances; for the time and your age.


un - one

deux - two

trois - three

quatre - four

cinq - five

six - six

sept - seven

huit - eight

neuf - nine

dix - ten

Did you notice that six is written the same in French as in English? It's not pronounced the same way though so be careful!

J’ai six ans. - I’m six years old.


Learning numbers in another language is not a very easy thing. The main problem is that most student "calculate" their French numbers: they think "twenty" and "five" to make "twenty five". But that is not how a native would think.

A native is so accustomed to numbers that "twenty five" is one piece of information.

Speed is also a big reason why students don’t understand French numbers – when numbers are all glided and meshed together, you have less time to think and break them apart, and it can get challenging.

Don’t worry though, I promise you that once you’ve trained with my lesson, speed is not going to be a problem anymore.

Start with the beginning – conquering the smaller digits will unlock large numbers for you.

The pronunciation of French numbers is very straightforward. French numbers build on each in almost the same way that they do in English. Therefore, once you get to twenty, it's pretty smooth sailing.

This can be practiced by squeezing the nose with thumb and forefinger as you pronounce the letters, shortly and lightly.

In English, numbers start building after 12. This is not the case in French. Numbers are distinct until 17, which is when they start building by putting together 10 (dix) and 7 (sept.)
